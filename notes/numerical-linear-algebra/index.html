  • The unit roundoff uuu for floating-point arithmetic is half the gap between 111 and the next largest floating-point number. It is u=2−53≈10−16u = 2^{-53} \approx 10^{-16}u=2−53≈10−16 for double-precision (525252 bits of precision). The relative error after an elementary floating point operation is bounded above by uuu.   • LU decomposition
  • For a square matrix AAA, we can often compute its LU decomposition
  • A=LU A = LU A=LU
  • where LLL is unit lower-triangular and UUU is upper-triangular. This factorization is an algebraic description of Gaussian elimination. With the LU decomposition A=LUA = LUA=LU, solving Ax=bAx = bAx=b becomes simple. We first solve Ly=bLy = bLy=b by forward substitution, then Ux=yUx = yUx=y by backsubstitution, both with complexity O(n2)O(n^2)O(n2).
  • To upper-triangularize AAA, we repeatedly apply Gauss transformations, where the kkkth Gauss transformation GkG_kGk​ zeroes out the lower part of the kkkth column.   • The kkkth Gauss transformation is an outer product update Gk=I−gkekTG_k = I - g_k e_k^TGk​=I−gk​ekT​ where the vector gkg_kgk​ has components gk[i]={0i≤kv[i]v[k]i>k, g_k[i] = \begin{cases} 0 & i \le k \\ \frac{v[i]}{v[k]} & i > k \end{cases}, gk​[i]={0v[k]v[i]​​i≤ki>k​,
  • and vvv is the kkkth column of Gk−1⋯G1A=Uk−1G_{k-1} \cdots G_1 A = U_{k-1}Gk−1​⋯G1​A=Uk−1​. The entry v[k]v[k]v[k] is called the pivot and therefore must be non-zero. Conveniently, LkL_kLk​ has the form Lk=[1∣1∣∣1∣∣∣1g1⋯gk⋱∣∣∣1].   • We repeat this, if possible, for k=1,…,n−1k = 1, \ldots, n-1k=1,…,n−1 to obtain the LU decomposition. The kkkth pivot is non-zero if and only if the first kkk-by-kkk submatrix of AAA is invertible. Therefore, the LU factorization exists if and only if this condition is satisfied for k=1,…,n−1k = 1, \ldots, n-1k=1,…,n−1. If AAA itself is also invertible, then the LU factorization is unique.
  • Pivots that are equal to or close to zero present numerical difficulties (i.e. LU is not backward stable). To avoid this, before the kkkth Gauss transformation, we can exchange rows and/or columns so that the pivot becomes larger (by magnitude). This computes the decomposition PAQ=LUPAQ = LUPAQ=LU, where PPP permutes rows and QQQ permutes columns. In this case, the decomposition always exists (if AAA is invertible).
  • There are three common strategies for choosing a row and/or column to swap the kkkth row and/or column with:
    • Partial pivoting: find the largest (by magnitude) entry within A[k:n,k]A[k:n, k]A[k:n,k]
    • Complete pivoting: find the largest entry within A[k:n,k:n]A[k:n, k:n]A[k:n,k:n]
    • Rook pivoting: search A[k:n,k:n]A[k:n, k:n]A[k:n,k:n] for an entry that is the largest within its row and column by starting from A[k,k]A[k,k]A[k,k] and alternately ascending within its row, then its column, etc. until convergence
  • With any of these strategies, the entries of LLL will have magnitude at most 111, leading to a stable algorithm.
  • In the LU algorithm (with or without pivoting), the naive algorithm requires O(n2k)O(n^2k)O(n2k) time for kkk steps, the same as Gaussian elimination’s O(n3)O(n^3)O(n3) for nnn steps. However, in partial or rook pivoting, we can reduce this to O(nk2)O(nk^2)O(nk2) if we assume that the pivot is found within O(1)O(1)O(1) linear searches. In complete pivoting, we can stop once the pivot is approximately 000, indicating the rank of the matrix.
  • A useful tool in analysis is the Schur complement. In the partial decomposition [A11A12A21A22]=[L110L21I][U11U120U22], \begin{bmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} \\ A_{21} & A_{22} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} L_{11} & 0 \\ L_{21} & I \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} U_{11} & U_{12} \\ 0 & U_{22} \end{bmatrix}, [A11​A21​​A12​A22​​]=[L11​L21​​0I​][U11​0​U12​U22​​], it will turn out that U22=A22−A21A11−1A12U_{22} = A_{22} - A_{21}A_{11}^{-1}A_{12}U22​=A22​−A21​A11−1​A12​, the Schur complement of AAA.
  • Cholesky decomposition
  • If AAA is a symmetric positive-definite matrix, then there exists a unique lower-triangular matrix LLL with positive diagonal entries such that A=LLTA = LL^TA=LLT. This is the Cholesky decomposition, easily calculable from the LU decomposition. No pivoting will be required (because AAA is positive-definite), even for stability! Closely related is the LDL decomposition, A=LDLTA = LDL^TA=LDLT, where LLL is unit lower-triangular and DDD is diagonal.

  • Dense eigenvalue problems
  • There are many methods to find the eigenvectors of a matrix AAA. Once an eigenvector xxx is estimated, an estimate for the eigenvalue can be found by xTAxx^T A xxTAx for xxx normalized.
  • Power iteration finds the eigenvector with the largest eigenvalue by iterating x↦Axx \mapsto Axx↦Ax on a random vector, normalizing the vector after each step. The error decays as O(∣λ2λ1∣k)O(|\frac{\lambda_2}{\lambda_1}|^k)O(∣λ1​λ2​​∣k).
  • Inverse iteration finds the eigenvector with eigenvalue nearest a guess μ\muμ by applying power iteration to (A−μI)−1(A - \mu I)^{-1}(A−μI)−1. This works because A−μIA - \mu IA−μI has an eigenvector with eigenvalue approximately 000, namely the relevant eigenvector of AAA. The error decays as O(∣λ−μλ′−μ∣k)O(|\frac{\lambda - \mu}{\lambda' - \mu}|^k)O(∣λ′−μλ−μ​∣k) where λ′\lambda'λ′ is the next nearest eigenvalue.
  • Rayleigh quotient iteration works only on symmetric matrices. We perform inverse iteration as usual, but at every step, we set the guess eigenvalue μ\muμ with the latest estimated eigenvalue xTAxxTx\frac{x^T A x}{x^T x}xTxxTAx​. This yields cubic convergence, O(ϵ3k)O(\epsilon^{3k})O(ϵ3k). For symmetric matrices, it converges almost always, but the same is not true for non-symmetric matrices.
  • Orthogonal iteration is a batch version of power iteration. Let QQQ be an orthogonal basis for a subspace; then iterating AAA on QQQ, we should obtain an invariant subspace. Note that we are also implicitly iterating AAA on the nested subspaces Q[1:k]Q[1:k]Q[1:k] for all kkk. If we keep QQQ orthogonal at every step with the QR decomposition, we prevent the subspaces from collapsing into fewer dimensions. If Q0Q_0Q0​ has rrr columns, the convergence to the correct subspace is O(∣λr+1λr∣k)O(|\frac{\lambda_{r+1}}{\lambda_r}|^k)O(∣λr​λr+1​​∣k).
  • Since a fixed point of this iteration is A=QTQTA = Q T Q^TA=QTQT for TTT upper-triangular, we see that this algorithm computes the Schur decomposition of AAA. Since TTT is similar to AAA, they have the same eigenvalues, and since it is triangular, they appear on the diagonal. Thus, we can interpret the entries on the diagonal of QkTAQk=TkQ_k^T AQ_k = T_kQkT​AQk​=Tk​ as approximations to the eigenvalues of AAA, even though the columns of QkQ_kQk​ are not necessarily eigenvectors (unless AAA is symmetric).
  • Notice that we can compute Tk+1T_{k+1}Tk+1​ directly from TkT_kTk​.   • There are several optimizations that make this algorithm practical:
    • Reduction to upper-Hessenberg form: In the general case, each step takes O(n3)O(n^3)O(n3) time, prohibitively expensive. However, we can preconvert A=QTHQA = Q^T H QA=QTHQ into upper-Hessenberg form by conjugating with n−2n - 2n−2 Householder reflections, in O(n3)O(n^3)O(n3) operations. Then at each QR step, we can compute the QR decomposition with only n−1n-1n−1 Givens rotations, so that each step requires only O(n2)O(n^2)O(n2) operations. Each QR step preserves the upper-Hessenberg form (Tk+1=RTkR−1T_{k+1} = R T_k R^{-1}Tk+1​=RTk​R−1).
    • Deflation: If at some point TkT_kTk​ becomes unreduced upper-Hessenberg (that is, one of the subdiagonal entries becomes 000), then the matrix becomes block upper-triangular, and the eigenvalues of the whole matrix can be found by finding the eigenvalues of the diagonal blocks.
    • Bulge chasing: At each QR step, the usual method is to calculate Givens rotations G1,…,Gn−1G_1, \ldots, G_{n-1}G1​,…,Gn−1​ and apply them first on the left and then on the right: Tk+1=((Gn−1T⋯(G1TTk))G1)⋯Gn−1.   • Sparse eigenvalue problems
  • When applying QR iteration to sparse matrices, reduction to upper-Hessenberg form using Householder reflections destroys sparsity. Instead, it is possible to achieve the reduction iteratively using the Gram-Schmidt process.
  • The kkkth Krylov subspace of a matrix AAA and a starting vector bbb is Kk(A,b)=span{b,Ab,…,Ak−1b}. K_k(A,b) = \mathrm{span}\,\{ b, Ab, \ldots, A^{k-1}b \}. Kk​(A,b)=span{b,Ab,…,Ak−1b}.
  • Arnoldi iteration calculates an orthogonal basis Qk={q1,…,qk}Q_k = \{ q_1, \ldots, q_k \}Qk​={q1​,…,qk​} that spans Kk(A,b)K_k(A,b)Kk​(A,b) via modified Gram-Schmidt. QkTAQkQ_k^T A Q_kQkT​AQk​ is upper-Hessenberg by construction (see below), and we can use its eigenvalues to approximate the eigenvalues of AAA. These eigenvalues are called Ritz eigenvalues and often converge to the extremal eigenvalues of AAA. Therefore, after kkk steps of Arnoldi iteration, we may apply the usual QR algorithm to the kkk-by-kkk upper-Hessenberg matrix to find the extremal eigenvalues of AAA.
  • To obtain qk+1q_{k+1}qk+1​, it suffices to orthonormalize AqkAq_{k}Aqk​ instead of AkbA^{k}bAkb, since Kk+1(A,b)=span{b,Aq1,…,Aqk}. K_{k+1}(A,b) = \mathrm{span}\,\{ b, Aq_1, \ldots, A q_{k} \}. Kk+1​(A,b)=span{b,Aq1​,…,Aqk​}. To see this, note that Akb=A(Ak−1b)=A(a1q1+⋯+akqk)=a1Aq1+⋯+akAqk. A^k b = A(A^{k-1}b) = A(a_1q_1 + \cdots + a_k q_k) = a_1 Aq_1 + \cdots + a_k Aq_k. Akb=A(Ak−1b)=A(a1​q1​+⋯+ak​qk​)=a1​Aq1​+⋯+ak​Aqk​. By induction, only the AqkAq_kAqk​ component can be orthogonal to Kk(A,b)K_k(A,b)Kk​(A,b), so we may consider only it instead of AkbA^k bAkb.
  • Since qk+1q_{k+1}qk+1​ is orthonormalized against Aq1,…,Aqk−1Aq_1, \ldots, Aq_{k-1}Aq1​,…,Aqk−1​, this means that Hk=QkTAQkH_k = Q_k^T A Q_kHk​=QkT​AQk​ is upper-Hessenberg, and indeed, so is H~k=Qk+1TAQk\tilde{H}_k = Q_{k+1}^T A Q_kH~k​=Qk+1T​AQk​. Note that QkTQk=IQ_k^T Q_k = IQkT​Qk​=I, but QkQkTQ_k Q_k^TQk​QkT​ is a projection into Kk(A,b)K_k(A,b)Kk​(A,b). Thus we may write Qk+1H~k=AQkQ_{k+1} \tilde{H}_k = AQ_kQk+1​H~k​=AQk​.
  • Lanczos iteration is the same process for symmetric matrices. In this case, Hk=QkTAQkH_k = Q_k^T A Q_kHk​=QkT​AQk​ is symmetric as well and thus tridiagonal. Remarkably, this means that in the Lanczos basis, AqkAq_kAqk​ only has components in the qk−1q_{k-1}qk−1​, qkq_kqk​, and qk+1q_{k+1}qk+1​ directions. Hence, in each Gram-Schmidt step, we need to subtract the projections onto qk−1q_{k-1}qk−1​ and qkq_kqk​. This works in theory, but when using inexact arithmetic, the calculated vectors do not tend to stay orthogonal to each other.
  • Lanczos iteration terminates in at most ppp steps, where ppp is the number of distinct eigenvalues of AAA. To see this, decompose bbb into its components in the ppp eigenspaces. The powers AkbA^k bAkb can be written as a linear combination of these kkk components, so Kk(A,b)K_k(A,b)Kk​(A,b) can grow to be at most ppp-dimensional.
  • Each iteration requires a matrix-vector product and orthogonalizing against 222 vectors. Thus, the work per iteration is O(n)O(n)O(n) plus the work required for a matrix-vector product (which may be cheap, since AAA is usually sparse).
  • Sparse linear systems
  • Sparse matrix formats
  • For sparse direct methods, it’s important how the matrices are stored. The compressed sparse row (CSR) format allow efficient matrix-vector multiplications and row access. It keeps a list of non-zero entries and their column numbers in row-major order and a list of row pointers that indicate the start of each row in the first list. The compressed sparse column (CSC) format is analogous for columns.
  • Direct methods
  • Sparse triangular solve
  • The most basic ingredient for solving sparse linear systems is solving a triangular system Lx=bLx = bLx=b, where LLL and bbb are both sparse. This is done in two steps: determining which entries of xxx can be non-zero and then computing the actual numerical solution.
  • To compute which entries of xxx can be non-zero, note that the solution via forward substitution is xi=1ℓii[bi−∑j=1i−1ℓijxj], x_i = \frac{1}{\ell_{ii}} \Big[ b_i - \sum_{j=1}^{i-1} \ell_{ij} x_j \Big], xi​=ℓii​1​[bi​−j=1∑i−1​ℓij​xj​], so we find two rules:
    • if bib_ibi​ is non-zero, then xix_ixi​ may be non-zero;
    • if ℓij\ell_{ij}ℓij​ and xjx_jxj​ are both non-zero, then xix_ixi​ may be non-zero.
  • The reach of a bib_ibi​ is the set of entries xjx_jxj​ that may be non-zero if bib_ibi​ is non-zero. We can visualize these rules in two ways.
  • Visually on the matrix LLL, to find the reach of bib_ibi​, first mark xix_ixi​. Now trace down the iiith column of the matrix, and if the kkkth entry is non-zero, then mark xkx_kxk​. Then repeat down the kkkth column, for every entry so marked, recursively until out of columns to trace.
  • More abstractly, we can consider the graph whose adjacency matrix is LLL, directed low to high. Then the reach of bib_ibi​ is the set of nodes reachable from the node iii.
  • Cholesky decomposition for sparse matrices
  • If AAA is symmetric positive-definite, we can solve linear systems using the Cholesky decomposition.
  • To derive the up-looking Cholesky factorization, we note that it must satisfy [A11a21a21Ta22]=[L110ℓ21Tℓ22][L11Tℓ210ℓ22]. \begin{bmatrix} A_{11} & a_{21} \\ a_{21}^T & a_{22} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} L_{11} & 0 \\ \ell_{21}^T & \ell_{22} \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} L_{11}^T & \ell_{21} \\ 0 & \ell_{22} \end{bmatrix}.     • L11L_{11}L11​ may be solved recursively from A11A_{11}A11​,
    • ℓ21\ell_{21}ℓ21​ is a sparse triangular solve involving the corresponding row a21a_{21}a21​, and
    • ℓ22\ell_{22}ℓ22​ involves a sparse dot product.
  • Thus each row of LLL is a sparse triangular solve involving the submatrix above it, allowing us to compute LLL row-by-row. We can think of the resulting sparsity pattern of LLL as a “filling in” of AAA.
  • When we calculate the sparsity pattern of the kkkth row ℓk\ell_kℓk​ (according to Lk−1ℓk=akL_{k-1} \ell_k = a_kLk−1​ℓk​=ak​), we would normally compute the graph of Lk−1L_{k-1}Lk−1​ and compute the reach of aka_kak​ in it. However, here we can leverage the special structure of the problem. There are two optimizations:
    • Lk−1L_{k-1}Lk−1​ is a submatrix of LkL_kLk​, so when we build the graph of LkL_kLk​, we may simply take the graph of Lk−1L_{k-1}Lk−1​ and add on the edges arising from the entries in ℓk\ell_{k}ℓk​.
    • Instead of using the full graph, we may consider only the elimination tree, which is the subgraph of the full adjacency graph that indicates only the first non-diagonal entry of each column. A node in the elimination tree has the same reach as in the full graph of LkL_kLk​: suppose two entries in the same column, ℓji\ell_{ji}ℓji​ and ℓki\ell_{ki}ℓki​, are non-zero. When computing the kkkth row, i→ji \to ji→j exists, so the ℓki\ell_{ki}ℓki​ would force the fill-in of ℓkj\ell_{kj}ℓkj​. If ℓkj\ell_{kj}ℓkj​ is the first entry in its column, then an j→kj \to kj→k edge exists, making the i→ki \to ki→k edge redundant. Otherwise, we may repeat the argument to show that a j⇝kj \rightsquigarrow kj⇝k path exists, again making the i→ki \to ki→k edge redundant.
  • Splitting methods
  • To solve Ax=bAx = bAx=b, we can split A=M−NA = M - NA=M−N for MMM invertible.
